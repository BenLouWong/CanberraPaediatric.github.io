Extractions

A dental extraction is the removal of one or more teeth from the mouth. Conditions requiring an extraction include:

  • Non-restorable deep decay infecting the nerves and blood vessels
  • Orthodontics extraction (braces) required to help with teeth and jaw alignment
  • Teeth overcrowding
  • Wisdom Teeth Extractions
  • Periodontal Disease
  • Traumatised Teeth

    • Dental extraction is often the last resort and we will always make sure it’s something you and your child are comfortable with. We will communicate clearly throughout the procedure and discuss all available options to help you make the right choice.

    Dental Fillings

    Fillings are a common type of restorative treatment used to fill a cavity or hole in a tooth caused by decay or previous extraction. For children’s teeth, we offer composite resin for fillings. They offer natural-looking appearances and can create a strong bond with the teeth to help with restoration. Reasons a child may need a filling includes:

  • Damage from trauma
  • Decay and cavity
  • Incorrect dental development
  • Root Canal Treatment
  • Discolouration

    • Root Canal Therapy

    A root canal is a safe and effective way to save a badly infected or damaged tooth. As opposed to an extraction, a root canal does not remove the tooth, but rather cleans out the diseased pulp around it and reshapes the canal.

    Your child might require root canal treatement if they exhibit any of the following

  • Toothache from biting, chewing or external pressure
  • Toothache or sensitivity aggravated by hot or cold temperatures
  • Darkening of one or more teeth
  • Swelling and tenderness in the gums
  • Swelling of the face or neck
  • Unexpected looseness of one or more teeth

    • Widom Teeth Removal

    Wisdom teeth, also known as third molars, are the last teeth to grow in the mouth, usually emerging in late teen years or early twenties. They appear behind the chewing teeth at the back of the mouth. In most cases, wisdom teeth erupt without causing significant discomfort in the mouth. However, there are some situations in which they need to be removed indefinitely, such as potential for impacted wisdom teeth (when a tooth gets stuck under the gum), crowding, pain, infection, damage to surrounding molars and nerves.

    Diagnosis of Dental disease

    The predominant disease that affects the dentitions f children is dental decay. Dental decay or cavities can lead to pain and or infection. Dental decay can affect the ability of a child to eat or drink. In very serious circumstance the decayed tooth can lead to a facial swelling which can be distressing for the child and the family.

    Diagnosis of Dental anomalies

    Dental anomalies can affect the tooth number, shape, and form. These may range for extra teeth to missing teeth. Anomalies of tooth number can affect the occlusion and may require interceptive orthodontic treatment. Anomalies in form may affect cosmetics and development of the jaws. Poorly formed enamel of hypomineralised teeth can often be misdiagnosed as tooth decay, children who present with unusual enamel formation should have a correct diagnosis before commencement of management.
